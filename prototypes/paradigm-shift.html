Dead End
Gavin Andresen is one of the most technically sophisticated people in Bitcoin — handpicked by Satoshi himself as lead developer. He ran his own verification process during a private signing session in London.
  • Jon Matonis, director of the Bitcoin Foundation, was also in the room and was independently convinced
  • Ian Grigg, a respected cryptographer, was present and corroborated the session
  • Gavin paid an enormous personal price for his claim — stripped of his Bitcoin GitHub access, ostracized by the community
Believing Gavin was simply "tricked" requires three independent, technically sophisticated witnesses to all have been fooled simultaneously. Possible, but deeply unsatisfying.
Hypothesis 02
Vitalik is wrong
Craig Wright really is Satoshi Nakamoto
Click to explore →
Dead End
The evidence against Wright acting alone as Satoshi has only grown stronger over the years:
  • The public verification failed before Vitalik made his statement — he was reacting to evidence
  • Wright was found to have fabricated evidence in court during the COPA trial
  • His technical knowledge shows visible gaps — multiple public instances of errors on cryptographic fundamentals
  • His Australian time zone doesn't match Satoshi's posting patterns
  • Satoshi coded on Windows — Wright is not a Windows user
  • Years after the ATO meeting, Wright still never proved he controlled the genesis block keys
Too much stacks against Vitalik being wrong. Another dead end.

Dead End
If Gavin was corrupt, what did he gain? The answer is: nothing. He lost everything.
  • His commit access to the Bitcoin GitHub repository was revoked
  • He was effectively expelled from the Bitcoin community he helped build
  • His reputation took severe damage — years of credibility spent in a single statement
  • He had no financial incentive — no known business ties to Wright at the time
A corrupt actor acts for gain. Gavin gained nothing and lost nearly everything. Dead end.
Hypothesis 04
The proof was faked entirely
Nothing real happened in that private session
Click to explore →
Dead End
But three credible people were in that room — not just Gavin.
  • Jon Matonis, director of the Bitcoin Foundation, independently stated he was convinced
  • Ian Grigg, a respected cryptographer in the Bitcoin community, corroborated the session
  • Gavin has consistently maintained — for years, at great personal cost — that what he witnessed was convincing
Dismissing the private session entirely requires believing that all three witnesses were either fooled by the same trick or colluding in a conspiracy. At best, what Wright demonstrated was partial control — enough to convince, but not enough to constitute full proof. Something real happened in that room. But what exactly?
Hypothesis 05
Satoshi Nakamoto was a team
What if we're asking the wrong question?

Every door opens
If Satoshi Nakamoto was not one person but a team, then suddenly every contradiction resolves:
  • Gavin can be right — Wright was involved. He had access to some keys, enough to demonstrate partial control in a private session
  • Vitalik can also be right — Wright is not the Satoshi, because there is no single Satoshi
  • The partial proof makes sense — a team member wouldn't possess all the keys, only their share
  • The technical gaps make sense — Wright's expertise was only partial because his role was only partial
  • The time zone mismatch makes sense — other team members in different time zones posted as Satoshi
  • The failed public proof makes sense — Wright couldn't prove full control because he never had full control
The binary question — "Is Craig Wright Satoshi?" — was always the wrong question. The right question is: "Was Craig Wright part of the team that created Bitcoin?"
The frame shifts.
Every dead end you encountered above was caused by the same assumption: that Satoshi Nakamoto is one person. Remove that assumption, and the evidence that contradicted itself suddenly tells a coherent story.
Reframing the evidence
× "He failed to prove he's Satoshi" → He couldn't prove sole ownership because he was never the sole owner
× "His technical knowledge has gaps" → His knowledge was partial because his role was partial
× "The private session was convincing" → He did have access to some keys — just not all of them
× "Gavin threw away his career for this" → Gavin saw something real — he just drew too broad a conclusion
